```latex
\subsection{Statistical Significance and Power}

Directional-accuracy comparisons at this sample size require care. With
$n=120$ walk-forward steps per ticker, the standard error on a single accuracy
estimate is $\sqrt{0.25/120}=4.56$ percentage points, so the per-ticker figures in
Table~\ref{tab:analyst} carry 95\% intervals roughly $\pm 9$ points wide --- wide
enough that no individual ticker's accuracy is distinguishable from chance.

Pooling all five tickers ($n=600$) reduces the standard error to 2.04 points. At
that sample size Ridge's 55.7\% average is significantly above the 50\% random-walk
baseline (95\% CI $[51.7, 59.7]$\%, $z=2.79$, $p=0.005$), whereas the
Ridge-plus-classifier ensemble's 53.5\% is not (95\% CI $[49.5, 57.5]$\%,
$p=0.086$). The gradient-boosted variant's 3.9-point margin over Ridge on the
held-out split corresponds to $p=0.056$ under a paired comparison, and to
$p=0.22$ after Bonferroni correction for the four model variants we evaluated.
Detecting a 3.9-point difference at 80\% power would require approximately 1{,}290
walk-forward steps; we have 600.

The same arithmetic bounds what the portfolio results in Table~\ref{tab:portfolio}
can establish. Estimating the variance of a Sharpe-ratio difference between two
correlated return series via the Jobson--Korkie statistic with Memmel's
correction, and measuring the strategy--benchmark correlation directly from our
own backtest ($\rho \approx 0.55$), detecting a true Sharpe improvement of $+0.30$
at 80\% power would require on the order of $10^4$ trading days per ticker. Over a
five-year window the corresponding power is under 10\%. We therefore present the
drawdown reduction --- which is large, consistent in sign across all five tickers,
and does not depend on resolving a small mean difference --- as our substantive
risk-management finding, and treat the return and Sharpe comparisons as
descriptive rather than inferential.

We note this constraint applies broadly rather than only to our system: evaluations
in this literature commonly report a few months to two years of daily data on a
handful of correlated large-capitalisation equities, a regime in which
conventional significance thresholds cannot be met for effects of the size
typically claimed.
```

Two limitations are statistical rather than architectural. First, the evaluation
universe is five large-capitalisation US technology stocks selected ex post; they
are mutually correlated and survivorship-selected, so cross-ticker averages
overstate the effective sample size. Second, and more fundamentally, the
sample is too small to support inferential claims about risk-adjusted return
(Section~VI-E). Readers should treat the return and Sharpe figures in
Table~\ref{tab:portfolio} as descriptive of this particular window. We report them
in full, including where the strategy underperforms, precisely because selective
reporting at this sample size is how spurious findings enter the literature.
